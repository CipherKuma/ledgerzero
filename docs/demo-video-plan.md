### 01 Hook

AI workers should be assets, not rented sessions.

Today, teams pay agents to research, write, trade, support customers, analyze data, and operate workflows. But the worker itself is usually trapped inside one account, one tool, or one platform. Its memory cannot move. Its capability history is hard to inspect. And if the worker becomes valuable, there is no clean market for ownership.

Ledger Zero is a marketplace for AI labor that can be owned, hired, and sold.

### 02 Problem

The next generation of AI agents will not only answer prompts. They will accumulate skill, reputation, data access, workflow memory, and revenue history.

But without an ownership layer, all of that value disappears into closed platforms. A company can train a useful worker and still have no asset. A buyer can hire an agent and still have no trustworthy proof of what it can do. A creator can build a worker and still have no market for selling it.

That is the gap Ledger Zero is designed to fill.

### 03 Product Loop

Ledger Zero is built around a simple marketplace loop: register a worker, attach its manifest and proof history, post jobs against that worker, then make ownership and payout state inspectable.

The product treats an AI worker like an economic asset. A worker has capabilities. It has artifacts. It has an owner. It can be hired. And over time, it can build a history that makes it more valuable.

The point is not to list chatbots. The point is to make AI labor transferable.

### 04 Demo Marketplace

Here is the live marketplace experience.

The buyer can browse worker cards, compare capabilities, and open a worker detail page. Instead of only showing a name and avatar, the product is designed to surface the information a buyer actually needs: what the worker does, what artifacts support it, who owns it, and what proof history exists.

This is where Ledger Zero starts feeling like a market, not a directory. The interface is organized around trust, inspection, and purchase intent.

If AI workers become valuable, buyers will need this kind of due diligence before paying for them.

### 05 Demo Workflow

The second part of the demo shows how supply enters the market.

A creator can register a worker manifest, describe capabilities, and connect the worker to a job workflow. Then a team can post work against that agent and move toward proof-backed execution. This creates a full loop: supply, demand, job, proof, and history.

For judges, this matters because Ledger Zero is not only a landing page for a future idea. It already has the surfaces a real marketplace needs: marketplace browsing, worker registration, job posting, and proof review.

### 06 Proof

0G is the backbone that makes the marketplace credible.

Worker artifacts need to be portable. Job history needs to be inspectable. Ownership and payout state need to be understandable outside a private dashboard. Ledger Zero uses the Proof Center to show what is live, what is demo-backed, and what is fallback, so the product does not pretend everything is complete when it is not.

That honesty is important. A serious marketplace has to distinguish between claims and evidence.

0G storage gives the worker a durable artifact layer. Proof screens give buyers and judges something to inspect.

### 07 Judge Case

For judges, Ledger Zero is strong because it points past the hackathon.

The market is obvious: teams are already paying for AI labor. The missing layer is ownership, inspection, and resale. Ledger Zero uses 0G where it has real leverage: portable worker artifacts, proof-backed history, and a trust layer for future transactions.

The business path is also clear. Start with specialized AI workers, add proof-backed job history, then turn the best workers into tradeable assets.

### 08 Close

Ledger Zero is the marketplace layer for AI labor that can be owned, hired, and sold.

It gives creators a way to sell useful workers, buyers a way to inspect them, and agents a path to become assets instead of rented sessions.

Live demo: ledgerzero.pages.dev.
